The video first introduces three ways of accessing memory: **recall**, **recognition**, and **relearning**. These concepts can be easily understood with the example of school exams: fill-in-the-blank problems rely on recall, multiple-choice questions requires recognition, and reviewing old materials is the process of relearning.

Next, the video explains how memory is stored. Information first becomes **sensory memory**, and then is encoded in to **working memory**, and eventually either decays or is stored in **long-term memory**. Working memory involves both **explicit** and **implicit** processes. Long-term memory also comes in different forms, including **procedural** memory and **episodic** memory.

Finally, the video illustrates some memory tricks, such as **mnemonics** and **chunking**. It also elaborates on the difference between **shallow and deep processing**. Because deep processing requires one to understand the actual meaning instead of staying on the literal expression, the memory could be retained longer.

Eric believes it is because the education system starts looking for these kids too late. He thinks they should have to start finding when the kids are in fourth grade, the time when their talent shows up. > 7. Why do you think Carlos and the boy in fourth grade forget these events?

- For Carlos: In order to succeed in academic performance, he must suppress memories about shame (the oversized shoes) or overwhelm (taking care of his family). After putting everything else in a box, he could finally entirely focus on his studies.
- For the fourth-grade boy: He did not *forget* the memory. However, because the memory of the violence was too horrible, his mind detached the memory from his emotion.
